The dispute is not only about ethics language in policy statements; it is about who controls downstream behavior when a general-purpose model is integrated into defense planning and operational tooling. If vendors can define prohibited military use classes, they may create a private-layer safety regime that operates in parallel with state procurement authority; if governments can override those limits contractually, safety commitments risk becoming non-binding in high-stakes contexts. The practical governance challenge is therefore to align public accountability, vendor safety commitments, and mission-critical requirements without creating hidden policy by contract. ## Detailed Notes

- The conflict centers on enforceable use-boundary control, not just policy rhetoric.
- Procurement contracts are becoming a primary arena for AI safety governance.
- Vendor-side restrictions and state mission requirements can structurally collide.
- Autonomous-weapons adjacent model access is likely to require explicit risk-class definitions.
- Auditability and incident accountability clauses will become key contract terms.
- Policy-by-contract risk increases if public oversight lags behind bilateral agreements.
- This case may shape future norms for military AI deployment guardrails.
- A mixed governance model (technical safeguards + legal oversight) appears necessary.
