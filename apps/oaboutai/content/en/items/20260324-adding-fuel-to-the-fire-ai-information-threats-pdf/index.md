## Detailed Notes

- **Lifecycle threat framing:** The report organizes AI information threats into generation, dissemination, and engagement phases, showing how risk escalates when these are chained together.
- **Beyond deepfake-only narratives:** It broadens analysis to include synthetic text, manipulated framing, bot-amplified narratives, and AI-assisted persuasion tactics.
- **Crisis-specific vulnerability:** High-uncertainty events increase susceptibility to rapid misinformation uptake and reduce verification capacity among the public.
- **Compounding dynamics:** Harm is presented as multiplicative—misleading content + coordinated spread + interactive reinforcement creates stronger societal effects than any single vector.
- **Platform and aggregator risk:** Recommendation and aggregation systems can unintentionally intensify crisis misinformation if governance controls are weak.
- **Bot-network amplification:** Coordinated inauthentic behavior remains a central mechanism for scaling misleading narratives quickly.
- **Chatbot interaction risk:** Conversational systems can become engagement amplifiers, normalizing false narratives through repeated user interactions.
- **Data poisoning concern:** Corrupted or adversarial inputs can degrade model reliability in high-pressure information environments.
- **Defensive AI role:** The report recognizes AI can aid response through anomaly detection, prioritization, and support for rapid verification workflows.
- **No silver-bullet warning:** Detection tooling is useful but imperfect; transparency about false positives/negatives is treated as a governance requirement.
- **Preparedness over improvisation:** Predefined crisis protocols are recommended to reduce decision latency during active incidents.
- **Cross-sector coordination:** Effective response requires interoperable workflows across government agencies, platforms, and civil-society partners.
- **Accountability architecture:** The report calls for clearer ownership of decisions, escalation paths, and post-incident review mechanisms.
- **Exercise and rehearsal:** Regular drills are presented as necessary to validate readiness, not optional governance theater.
- **Public communication duty:** Clear, timely, and transparent messaging is positioned as a resilience control equal in importance to technical detection.
- **Strategic implication:** Institutions should build adaptive, continuously tested information-integrity systems rather than static anti-misinformation policies.
