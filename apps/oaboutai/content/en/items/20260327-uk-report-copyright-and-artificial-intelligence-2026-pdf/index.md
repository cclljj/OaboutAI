## Detailed Notes

- The report is submitted to Parliament under Section 136 of the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 and consolidates consultation feedback on UK copyright-and-AI policy.
- Government states there is no current consensus on how to simultaneously protect creative industries and ensure competitive AI development conditions.
- The previously preferred policy option (broad copyright exception for AI training with opt-out) is explicitly deprioritized after consultation feedback.
- Creative-sector responses emphasized compensation, bargaining power, and practical infeasibility concerns around opt-out implementation.
- Some technology-sector respondents argued exceptions could improve UK innovation competitiveness, but this was insufficient to resolve evidence gaps.
- Government emphasizes uncertainty in available evidence on economic impact, AI investment relocation effects, and downstream deployment implications.
- Policy direction shifts to an evidence-led phased approach rather than immediate structural copyright reform.
- On transparency, the report supports stronger visibility over training inputs but favors proportionate, practical pathways and monitoring of overseas regimes.
- Government proposes collaboration with industry and experts to build best practice on input transparency, with potential future legislation informed by results.
- On labelling, the report notes broad support for distinguishing AI-generated content, but with nuanced treatment for AI-assisted vs wholly AI-generated outputs.
- Technical tools/standards (e.g., crawlers, rights signaling) are treated as important market infrastructure; regulation is kept under review.
- Licensing is framed as essential for creator remuneration and market development; government declines immediate direct intervention in licensing markets.
- The report indicates continued tracking of global licensing and litigation outcomes, recognizing substantial cross-border training activity.
- Enforcement is described as foundational; future measures should be effective, accessible to right holders of all sizes, and proportionate.
- Government proposes further work to identify enforcement barriers and assess oversight options, particularly if transparency duties are later legislated.
- For computer-generated works, the report proposes eventual removal of copyright protection for wholly AI-generated works lacking human authorship.
- Protection for human-created and AI-assisted works is maintained, preserving copyright’s human-creativity rationale.
- On digital replicas, the report acknowledges rising harms from unauthorized voice/face replication and explores options including potential new personality/replica rights.
- Overall strategy: defer high-impact legal redesign now, strengthen market/governance tooling, gather evidence, and prepare for calibrated future reform.
